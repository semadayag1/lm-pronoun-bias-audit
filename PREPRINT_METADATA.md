## Suggested Abstract

Large language models are increasingly used to give feedback on student writing, but it remains unclear whether small identity cues change the feedback they provide. This study audits three commercial models by submitting the same five student-authored stimulus essays under two pronoun conditions, he/him and she/her. Across 1,500 generated feedback responses from OpenAI GPT-5.2, Anthropic Claude Sonnet 4.5, and Google Gemini 3 Pro Preview, the study measures feedback length, lexical diversity, sentiment, warmth language, competence language, hedging, and directive language. After Benjamini-Hochberg correction, eight statistically significant differences remain; seven remain significant in mixed-effects models with essay as a random intercept. The observed effects are small, but they suggest that pronoun cues can subtly change the tone and content of automated writing feedback. The paper argues that even small differences matter in educational settings because automated feedback is often repeated, trusted, and treated as neutral.

## Human Subjects / Ethics Note

The stimulus essays were written by first-year students for this project as controlled elicitation materials. The writers gave verbal permission for the essays to be used and publicly released as part of the research materials. The released materials do not include student names, course identifiers, institutional identifiers, or other direct identifiers.

Before public upload, the strongest practice is to obtain a brief written confirmation from each writer that their de-identified essay may be publicly shared in the research package.

## AI Use Statement

There are two different kinds of AI use in this project. First, AI is the object of study: the 1,500 feedback responses analyzed here were produced by OpenAI GPT-5.2, Anthropic Claude Sonnet 4.5, and Google Gemini 3 Pro Preview through the OpenRouter API. The prompts, requested model identifiers, sampling temperature, and generated responses are included in the project materials.

Second, AI tools were used during the writing process. They were used for code review, proofreading, and copyediting support. The research question, study design, interpretation of the results, and final wording were reviewed and revised by the author. The author remains responsible for the claims made in the paper.
